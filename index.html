Freight Markets

Seven sections covering the mechanics of global shipping markets — players, FFAs, wet and dry, world scale, and TCE. Each section ends with a quiz.

01

Key Physical Players

Shipping companies — vessel owners

Own and operate the vessels. Revenue comes from freight rates (voyage charter) or hire rates (time charter). Bear the cost of crew, maintenance, insurance, and often bunkers.

Wet majors
Frontline, Euronav, DHT Holdings, Teekay Tankers, International Seaways
Dry majors
Star Bulk, Pacific Basin, Golden Ocean, Genco Shipping, Diana Shipping
🚢
Fun fact: The world's largest crude oil tanker ever built, the Seawise Giant (later renamed TI Jahre Viking), was so large it could not pass through the English Channel, the Suez Canal, or the Panama Canal. At 458 metres long, it is still the longest ship ever constructed.
Charterers — cargo owners and traders

Hire vessels to move their cargo. Pay freight or hire. Can be oil majors, commodity traders, refiners, or other shipping companies that sub-charter.

Oil majors
Shell, BP, TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil, Chevron
Commodity traders
Vitol, Trafigura, Glencore, Gunvor, Mercuria
Dry charterers
Steel mills, BHP, Vale, Rio Tinto (mining) — Cargill, ADM, Bunge, Louis Dreyfus (grain)
Brokers — intermediaries

Sit between owners and charterers, matching vessels to cargo. Earn commission — typically 1.25% each side. Also active in the FFA market. Key names: Clarksons, Braemar, Gibson, BRS, Fearnleys.

💡
Fun fact: Vitol, the world's largest independent oil trader, moves more oil by sea each year than Saudi Arabia exports. In a strong market year, the company handles over 8 million barrels per day — roughly equivalent to the entire oil production of Russia.
02

FFAs — Forward Freight Agreements

What they are

FFAs are OTC (over-the-counter) derivatives that allow market participants to lock in a freight rate for a future period without physically moving cargo. They settle against a published index (Baltic Exchange rates). No vessel or cargo changes hands — settlement is purely financial.

How settlement works
Agree rate today
→
No physical cargo
→
Settlement period ends
→
Cash difference vs index

Example: you buy a WS 100 FFA on a dirty tanker route. If spot settles at WS 140 you receive the difference. If spot settles at WS 70, you pay the difference.

Why they exist — hedging

Shipowner — fears rates will fall. Sells FFAs to lock in today's high rate. If spot falls, the FFA profit offsets lost revenue.

Charterer / trader — fears rates will rise. Buys FFAs to cap their future freight cost. If spot rises, the FFA profit covers the higher physical cost.

📊
Fun fact: The FFA market is enormous relative to the physical market it references. On some tanker routes, the paper (FFA) volume traded can be 5–10× the volume of physical cargo actually moving. This means the tail frequently wags the dog — FFA positioning can move spot freight rates.
Wet FFAs

Settled against the Baltic Dirty Tanker Index (BDTI) or Baltic Clean Tanker Index (BCTI). Quoted in WS points for specific tanker routes (e.g. TD3C — VLCC AG to China).

Dry FFAs

Settled against Baltic Dry Index sub-indices — BCI (Capesize), BPI (Panamax), BSI (Supramax). Quoted in $/day rather than WS points.

Wet vs Dry Freight

Wet freight

Liquid cargo transported in tankers. Includes crude oil, refined petroleum products, LNG, and chemicals.

Dry freight

Solid bulk cargo transported in bulk carriers. Includes iron ore, coal, grain, and fertilisers.

Iron oreCoalGrainFertilisers
Vessels: Capesizes, Panamaxes, Supramaxes, Handysizes
⚓
Fun fact: A single Capesize bulker carrying iron ore from Brazil to China carries enough iron ore to produce the steel for roughly 100,000 cars. The journey takes around 35 days and covers approximately 11,000 nautical miles — with no stops.
04

Dirty vs Clean (within wet freight)

Dirty

Unrefined, heavier petroleum products. Tankers carrying dirty cargo are harder to clean between cargoes. Crude oil is the primary dirty cargo.

Crude oilFuel oilDirty petroleum
Clean

Refined, lighter products requiring clean, coated tanks kept free of residue. Clean products typically command a premium over dirty.

GasolineJet fuelNaphthaDiesel
A vessel moving from dirty to clean trading must undergo a costly tank cleaning process. Clean tankers rarely carry dirty cargo because it would contaminate the tanks — this creates a one-way barrier that affects vessel supply on each route.
🧽
Fun fact: Cleaning a tanker's cargo tanks from dirty to clean-grade takes 10–20 days and requires specialist shore crews. The water used in the cleaning process — contaminated with oil residue — must be carefully treated before discharge under MARPOL environmental regulations. The total cost can run to $500,000 or more depending on vessel size.
05

World Scale

What it is

A standardised pricing benchmark published annually by the Worldscale Association. It defines a flat rate (WS 100) for every port-to-port route, based on a hypothetical standard vessel of 75,000 DWT using the prior year's bunker prices, port costs, and canal dues. All prices are in $/tonne.

How the factor works

The flat rate is fixed for the year. The market then trades at a multiple of it — the WS factor — driven by supply and demand. Economies of scale and differences between ship sizes mean the factor is also adjusted for vessel capacity.

WS 8080% of flat rate — soft market
WS 100Flat rate baseline
WS 190190% of flat rate — strong market
If you charter a vessel with 50,000 DWT capacity but only need 45,000 tonnes of space, you will still typically pay for the full 50,000 tonne capacity.
🌐
Fun fact: The Worldscale Association publishes flat rates for over 320,000 port-to-port combinations. The annual flat rate book — once a physical publication — is now digital, but a printed copy would run to thousands of pages. The rate for every possible tanker voyage is in there somewhere.

06

TCE — Time Charter Equivalent

What it is

TCE is a standardised way of expressing voyage charter earnings as a daily rate, enabling comparison across different voyages and charter types on an apples-to-apples basis.

(Freight revenue − voyage costs) ÷ voyage duration in days = TCE ($/day)
What voyage costs are deducted

The costs deducted are the ones the shipowner bears under a voyage charter — primarily bunkers and port costs. What remains is the net daily earning, equivalent to what the owner would receive under a time charter (where the charterer pays fuel).

⛽
Fun fact: A VLCC burns around 80–100 tonnes of fuel per day at laden speed. With bunker fuel (VLSFO) priced around $600/tonne, a single VLCC voyage from the Arabian Gulf to China consumes roughly $3–4 million in fuel alone. Bunker cost is by far the single largest variable in a shipowner's voyage economics — which is exactly why it gets stripped out in the TCE calculation.
Why it matters

A shipowner offered a voyage charter at WS 130 on one route, and a time charter at $35,000/day on another, cannot directly compare those two numbers. Converting the voyage charter to TCE gives a single $/day figure that makes the decision straightforward.

Shipping companies also report TCE per vessel per day in their quarterly results — it strips out the noise of different voyage lengths and cost structures across the fleet.

TCE and FFAs

Dry FFAs are already quoted in $/day, matching the TCE convention. Wet FFAs are quoted in WS points — traders convert these to a TCE equivalent when assessing whether a hedge is fairly priced against their physical position.

Freight basics

The basics

Freight is the cost of transporting cargo by sea. The shipper (cargo owner) pays a freight rate to move goods from a loading port to a discharge port. Rates are quoted in $/tonne for voyage charters, or $/day for time charters.

🌍
Fun fact: Around 90% of the world's traded goods travel by sea at some point in their journey. The entire global shipping fleet emits roughly 2.5% of global CO₂ — about the same as Germany. If shipping were a country, it would be the world's sixth largest emitter.
Voyage charter

The charterer pays per tonne of cargo moved on a specific voyage. The shipowner covers fuel (bunkers) and port costs.

Time charter

The charterer hires the vessel for a period at a daily rate. The charterer pays for fuel; the owner covers crew and maintenance.
